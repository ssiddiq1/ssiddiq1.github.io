About

Shayaan Siddique

Sophomore at Oberlin College studying Computer Science and Mathematics. I work at the intersection of quantitative finance and machine learning — building systems, doing research, and competing in mathematics.

Background

I'm based in New York and study at Oberlin College, where I'm double majoring in Computer Science and Mathematics with a 3.9 GPA. I founded Oberlin's Quantitative Trading Club and Consulting Club, and I'm a member of the Finance Club.

My academic focus is on the mathematical foundations that underpin both modern ML research and quantitative finance — group theory, linear algebra, discrete mathematics, and multivariable calculus. I like working on problems where rigorous math meets messy real-world data.

Research & ML

I independently developed a fine-tuned Llama model trained entirely on self-manufactured Putnam competition data. After publishing, I received unsolicited collaboration requests from OpenAI, Anthropic, and xAI. The model runs locally at 6× lower token usage than competing math models, and scored 6 points on Putnam problem B6.

I also built a sentiment pipeline (BERT/RoBERTa) for an early-stage multi-strategy hedge fund, processing 10,000+ earnings calls, filings, and news articles monthly — improving backtest accuracy by 23%. Separately, I built an AI-driven equity analytics pipeline generating alpha signals sold to hedge funds, automating SQL query generation and cutting compute costs by ~30%.

Finance

I've done buy-side valuation work with a senior PM at Lord Abbett in Global and EM Equities, preparing company research on Saint James Place (STJ.L). Earlier, I contributed to advisory work at NAS Economic Consulting on SEC regulatory matters — including a report on the Security-Based Swap Positions Rule for Elliott Investment Management and the JPMorgan vs. Tesla case.

I was selected for SIG's Discovery Day, engaging with traders and researchers on quantitative trading philosophy and market-making.

Mathematics

I ranked #1 nationally among sophomore participants in the 2025 William Lowell Putnam Mathematical Competition, and scored 2nd highest in the nation on problem B6 — the hardest problem on the exam.
