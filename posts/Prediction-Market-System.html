Quant Research Systems Day 0

Building an Autonomous Prediction Market Research System

At 7:24 PM I had a hypothesis and zero infrastructure. By midnight I had a live two-platform data collector, an autonomous researcher running hourly analysis, a real-time dashboard, and four genuine statistical findings. This is the story of how that happened and what the data says so far.

The hypothesis

Kalshi and Polymarket both offer 15-minute binary BTC contracts — a bet on whether Bitcoin's price will be higher or lower in the next 15 minutes. In an efficient market, two contracts covering identical outcomes should trade at identical prices. They don't.

I had noticed this watching the two platforms side by side. Kalshi showing YES at 85 cents while Polymarket showed the equivalent at 90 cents simultaneously. A 5-cent spread on a binary outcome that must resolve to either zero or one dollar. That gap shouldn't exist in theory. In practice it does, consistently.

The research question isn't "can I make money from this." It's why does this gap exist, how persistent is it, and what does it tell us about how information moves between prediction markets? That framing matters — it's the difference between a trading bot and a publishable research finding.

The technical architecture

The first challenge was getting the data. Kalshi has a clean REST API — the market endpoint returns bid/ask directly on the market object with no authentication required for market data. Polymarket is more involved: discovery through the Gamma API, token IDs from the CLOB, then separate bid and ask calls per token. The matching problem between the two platforms took the better part of the first hour.

The full system — quant-os/ — runs five agents off a single shell script.

collector.py
Kalshi data collector. Polls every 60 seconds. Queries the KXBTC15M series for the live contract and writes ticker, close_time, yes_bid, yes_ask, no_bid, no_ask, last_price, and 24h volume to SQLite.
polymarket_collector.py
Polymarket collector. Discovers active BTC 15-min contracts via the Gamma API using 15-minute epoch boundaries, resolves token IDs from the CLOB, and pulls bid/ask for the UP outcome. Same 60-second cadence.
researcher.py
Autonomous research agent. Runs every hour. Four statistical analyses, PNG charts, structured report to logs/reports/, permanent research log, and interactive [STRATEGY] / [RESEARCH] / [CODE] proposals.
ideator.py
Strategy ideation agent. Reads the research log and master prep document daily. Proposes updates to research direction and flags overdue items.
dashboard/app.py
Flask web dashboard. Live prices side-by-side, spread over time chart, collection health indicators, strategy tab with predictive model output, research history timeline. Auto-refreshes every 30 seconds.

Night one findings

After approximately three hours of data collection — 151 Kalshi rows and 42 Polymarket rows, 96 timestamp-matched pairs — the researcher ran its first full analysis. Here is what it found.

Systematic spread
−3.4¢

Kalshi trades at a consistent discount to Polymarket. Left-skewed — Polymarket occasionally spikes significantly higher. Shapiro-Wilk p=0.001.

Cross-correlation lag=0
0.950

Both markets move simultaneously with no detectable lead/lag at current sample size. Will be retested at 500+ matched rows.

Momentum lock-in
~8 min

Across 5 resolved windows, the leading probability reached 90%+ certainty at an average of 8 minutes into the 15-minute window.

Spread persistence
58.3%

When the spread exceeds 5 cents, it persists 5 minutes later 58% of the time. Fast mean-reversion is the dominant pattern.

The momentum finding

The 8-minute lock-in is the finding I find most interesting from a pure market microstructure perspective. Binary contracts are interesting because they compress all price information into a single probability. As a 15-minute window progresses, new information arrives — primarily actual BTC price movement — and the market updates accordingly.

What the data suggests, tentatively, is that by minute 8 the market has usually made up its mind. The leading probability has stabilized above 90% and stays there through resolution. A preliminary backtest of an entry-at-open, exit-at-minute-8 strategy on 8 resolved contracts showed a 62.5% win rate and average PnL of +$0.063 per contract. Far too small a sample to draw conclusions, but the direction is interesting.

The volatility correlation adds texture: the researcher found r=0.668 between realized BTC volatility and time-to-lock-in across 6 windows. Higher volatility windows take longer to converge. In a volatile environment the market is less certain about where BTC will land even as the window progresses, so the probability estimate stays uncertain longer.

Mean spread when BTC goes UP: +0.015. Mean spread when BTC goes DOWN: −0.037. Kalshi premium correlates with UP moves — r=0.471, p=0.239, n=8 windows. Not yet significant. Worth watching.

The audit trail

One design decision I'm glad I made: building the audit trail before the researcher. Every finding, proposal, and implementation decision gets timestamped and appended to a permanent research_log.md. The first entries look like this:

2026-04-09 02:10 [FINDING: SPREAD_DIST] Kalshi trades at systematic −0.034 discount to Polymarket. Not normally distributed. Left-skewed: Polymarket occasionally spikes high.
2026-04-09 02:10 [FINDING: VOL_LOCK] Pearson r=0.668 (p=0.147) between realized volatility and minutes to 90%+ lock-in across 6 windows. Higher volatility → slower convergence.
2026-04-09 02:20 [DECISION: YES] Implement tracking of spread sign vs BTC direction correlation. Added as Finding 5 in researcher.py.
2026-04-09 02:20 [IMPLEMENTED] Momentum backtest on 8 resolved contracts. Win rate 62.5%, avg PnL +$0.063/contract.

In six months this log becomes the raw material for the paper introduction. The narrative writes itself: here is what the system found on day one, here is how it evolved, here is what held up with more data and what didn't.

A structural caveat

One important note: Kalshi's KXBTC15M contract resolves YES if BTC is at or above a specific floor strike at expiration. Polymarket's contract resolves UP if BTC price rose from the window open. These are correlated but not identical payoff structures. The spread reflects both genuine mispricing and structural differences in what each contract measures.

Key open question

When Kalshi's floor strike equals the BTC price at contract open, the two contracts become functionally identical — and the spread in those windows would be pure mispricing. Isolating those cases systematically is the next analytical step.

What comes next

The system runs autonomously overnight. The questions that will likely have clearer answers by next week: does one market lead the other by 1–2 minutes? Does the spread sign predict BTC direction at 50+ windows? Does the 8-minute lock-in hold with 20+ resolved windows?

This is Day 0. The research log has its first entries. Everything after this is refinement.
