Quant Systems Research Policy

Building a Policy Market Alpha Engine

Most open quant projects are thin wrappers around price. This one is an attempt to build something categorically different: a self-improving research engine that ingests lobbying disclosures, federal contracts, insider filings, and other policy-adjacent public records, converts them into market-linked event streams, and evaluates them under an explicitly academic standard of evidence.

01 / Ingest

Public Policy Data

Lobbying disclosures, federal awards, insider ownership changes, regulatory events, and macro positioning.

02 / Resolve

Issuer Mapping

Messy legal entity strings are mapped to tickers, parents, sectors, and confidence-scored issuer IDs.

03 / Encode

Event Features

Every public record becomes a timestamped market event with lag-aware, reusable factorized features.

04 / Test

Alpha Validation

Walk-forward backtests, ablations, interaction tests, and regime-conditioned evaluation.

05 / Learn

Research Memory

Failed ideas are preserved, diagnosed, and reintroduced as constraints for future hypothesis generation.

The premise

The overwhelming majority of public “quant” repositories are not really information systems. They are transformations of price data. They compute indicators on OHLCV, fit a model, and then call the result a strategy. That can be useful as an exercise. It is not, in my view, a satisfying research agenda.

My starting point is simpler and more demanding: markets move because information arrives, gets processed unevenly, and only then gets expressed in price. If that is true, then a serious quant research system should spend at least as much effort modeling information formation and dissemination as it spends modeling candles.

The project described here — a Policy Market Alpha Engine — is an attempt to build exactly that kind of system. The engine ingests public records that sit one layer upstream of price discovery: lobbying filings, federal contract awards, insider ownership filings, and related policy or regulatory disclosures. It then performs entity resolution, event normalization, feature construction, hypothesis generation, backtesting, and research logging under a reproducible pipeline.

This framing is deliberate. The system is not a hard-coded trading bot with a few if-statements around an RSI threshold. It is much closer to an autonomous research process: propose a thesis, operationalize it, test it under temporal and statistical discipline, log the result, and use the outcome to refine the next generation of work.

The influence: autonomous research, not autonomous trading

The strongest conceptual inspiration for this system is the autonomous research-claw style of workflow associated with Andrew Karpathy’s broader view of coding agents and research automation: not “ask a model for an answer,” but rather build a machine that can structure inquiry. In the academic version of that idea, the pipeline looks like: 

topic → literature → synthesis → hypothesis → experiment → audit → revision

The policy alpha version substitutes financial event streams for literature and replaces the paper artifact with a ranked signal, a diagnostic report, and a durable research memory: 

public event → issuer mapping → feature set → hypothesis → backtest → robustness audit → archival memory

That similarity is not cosmetic. The most important property of a good autonomous research system is not that it can emit an answer quickly. It is that it can accumulate validated structure over time. The same idea applies here. An alpha engine that cannot remember what failed, why it failed, and under what regime assumptions it failed is not a research engine. It is a slot machine.

The information edge

The core bet is that policy-adjacent public records are underused in open quantitative research because they are ugly. They are sparse, irregular, text-heavy, issuer-ambiguous, and often expensive to normalize. That is exactly why they are interesting.

Consider three examples:

Lobbying
Intent before outcome. Lobbying disclosures expose who is spending to influence which issues and agencies. Even when noisy, they contain directional information about regulation, antitrust pressure, defense priorities, drug pricing pressure, export controls, spectrum allocation, and procurement focus.
Contracts
Revenue-like events before the narrative catches up. Federal contract awards are not “sentiment.” They are operational records with counterparties, amounts, agencies, and award timing. For the right names, these are real business events.
Insider Forms
Management behavior as a conditioning layer. A contract award or lobbying spike means something different when it occurs alongside clustered insider accumulation than when it occurs alongside broad insider selling.

Any one of these sources in isolation may be weak or regime-dependent. The real value appears when they are aligned in time, normalized to the same issuer universe, and tested not merely as raw features, but as interacting information channels.

System architecture

The system is organized as an event-native research stack rather than a chart-native trading stack. The distinction matters. Price is one downstream output. Events and entities are the primary units of analysis.

Data primitive
Event

Every source record is converted into a typed event with issuer ID, timestamp, payload, provenance, and confidence.

Research primitive
Hypothesis

Every candidate signal is expressed as a formal hypothesis with feature scope, lag assumptions, target, and evaluation window.

Memory primitive
Lesson

Every failed or unstable result is preserved as a lesson with explicit diagnosis rather than being silently discarded.

Validation primitive
Audit

Every accepted result survives timestamp correctness, walk-forward evaluation, and at least one robustness or ablation check.

1. Ingestion layer

The ingestion layer collects raw records from each public source and stores them without interpretation. That is a design choice. Early transformations tend to destroy provenance and make later debugging impossible.

Each ingestor writes append-only tables with: 

source_id
raw_entity_name
raw_timestamp
retrieval_timestamp
document_id
payload_json
checksum
schema_version

The retrieval timestamp is as important as the document timestamp. In event-driven finance, timing assumptions are not a footnote; they are the difference between valid and invalid research.

2. Entity resolution layer

Public records rarely hand you a clean ticker. They give you subsidiary names, legal names, acronyms, historical names, or registrants that only indirectly identify the market-facing issuer. A meaningful fraction of the engineering work is therefore not “ML,” but entity hygiene.

The resolver maintains a canonical issuer graph: 

canonical_issuer_id
issuer_name
ticker
sector
industry
aliases[]
subsidiaries[]
parent_id
resolution_confidence

Mapping is done through staged resolution:

Stage A Deterministic matching. Exact alias and normalized legal-name matches.
Stage B String similarity. Token normalization, edit-distance heuristics, suffix stripping, corporate designator handling.
Stage C Contextual resolution. Counterparty context, agency context, sector priors, and document co-occurrence features.
Stage D Human-review queue. Low-confidence mappings are retained but quarantined from production research paths.

This is one of the few parts of the stack where correctness matters more than model sophistication. A beautiful model trained on bad issuer mappings is simply a mechanism for generating confidence in noise.

3. Event normalization

Once issuers are resolved, each source record is transformed into a typed event object. The engine does not care whether the original source was a lobbying filing or a contract award PDF. After normalization, it cares about event semantics: 

{
  "event_type": "lobbying_issue_update",
  "issuer_id": "issuer_4831",
  "ticker": "LMT",
  "sector": "Industrials",
  "effective_timestamp": "2026-04-09T00:00:00Z",
  "availability_timestamp": "2026-04-10T13:41:12Z",
  "source": "LDA",
  "payload": {
    "issue_cluster": "defense_procurement",
    "reported_amount": 280000,
    "agency_targets": ["DOD", "Congress"]
  },
  "confidence": 0.94
}

This allows feature generation to be source-agnostic. The feature engine consumes event streams, not bespoke source tables.

4. Feature store

The feature store is not a flat matrix dumped from pandas. It is a time-aware, entity-aware layer with explicit lagging rules. Every feature is generated with a known observation window and a known earliest usable timestamp.

Feature family Lobby intensity delta

Quarter-over-quarter or rolling-window changes in lobbying spend, issue breadth, and agency target concentration.

Feature family Contract surprise ratio

Contract size normalized by market cap, historical award cadence, and issuer baseline procurement footprint.

Feature family Cross-source agreement

Interaction terms measuring whether independent public channels align on the same issuer or sector at the same time.

Examples of the first feature families: 

lobby_spend_delta_qoq_sector
issue_cluster_entropy_90d
contract_award_surprise_zscore
contract_value_to_market_cap
insider_net_buy_flag_30d
policy_alignment_score
cross_source_agreement_score
regime_conditioned_pressure_index

Note the pattern: these are not technical indicators. They are operational summaries of public information flow.

The academic lens

This is the part I care most about. The value of the system is not that it automates ingestion or writes a nice report. The value is that it enforces a research process that is much closer to an academic workflow than to the usual hobbyist quant loop of “fit until it looks good.”

Every candidate signal begins as a formal hypothesis: 

H1:
An increase in antitrust-related lobbying intensity for mega-cap platform companies,
conditioned on elevated insider selling and high prior valuation dispersion,
predicts 5-day relative underperformance versus sector ETF benchmarks.

That hypothesis can then be decomposed:

Target 5-day excess return relative to sector ETF or industry basket.
Features Lobby intensity, issue cluster score, insider overlay, dispersion regime.
Availability Lagged to first public availability timestamp.
Evaluation Walk-forward with cost assumptions and subsample robustness checks.

This sounds obvious, but it is surprisingly rare in open trading code. Most projects never elevate a signal to the status of a proposition that can be falsified. They only ask whether a backtest is green. That is not enough. A useful research system must be able to say not just what worked, but why it was thought to work, under what assumptions, and how it failed when those assumptions broke.

Research standard

The engine treats positive results as provisional and negative results as informative. A signal that fails stability testing but reveals a strong cross-source interaction is not wasted effort; it becomes structured evidence for the next round of hypothesis construction.

The self-improving loop

The self-improving component is not a decorative “AI” label. It is a concrete memory mechanism. Each run generates not only results, but diagnoses: 

[FINDING] contract_award_surprise has positive drift in defense subsector
[WARNING] signal collapses after lag-corrected availability adjustment
[INSIGHT] interaction with insider accumulation remains significant
[REJECTED] standalone lobbying spend feature unstable across election-cycle subsamples
[LESSON] prefer interaction terms over single-source event scores in sparse policy domains

Those lessons are written to a persistent research ledger. Future runs use them as constraints. The system can therefore improve in three distinct senses:

Search quality
↑

Failed feature families are deprioritized while stable interaction motifs are upweighted in future hypothesis generation.

Statistical hygiene
↑

Pathologies such as timing leakage, overconcentration, or regime fragility become named failure modes instead of recurring mistakes.

Interpretability
↑

The system accumulates a library of causal stories and counterexamples rather than an unstructured pile of model checkpoints.

Research velocity
↑

Future experiments begin from a better prior because the engine remembers what the prior round learned.

That, to me, is the most important inheritance from autonomous academic systems. The point is not merely to automate a workflow. The point is to turn iteration into compounding knowledge.

Backtesting discipline

Event-driven policy research is uniquely vulnerable to false positives because timestamps are messy and publication delays are not optional. A contract award can be “effective” on one date, reported on another date, and retrieved by your collector on a third. The system therefore distinguishes: 

effective_timestamp
public_availability_timestamp
retrieval_timestamp
trade_eligibility_timestamp

Any backtest that collapses those into one date is structurally suspect. The engine’s validation layer therefore includes:

Walk-forward
Strict temporal splits. No global fitting over the full sample before evaluation.
Lag audit
Availability-aware execution. Signals can only act after a source-specific minimum delay.
Ablation
Source and interaction testing. Remove one feature family at a time to see where signal quality actually comes from.
Subsamples
Regime checking. Election-cycle, high-volatility, sector-specific, and liquidity-conditioned tests.

This is what separates the system from a fixed rules engine. The objective is not to emit a trade at all costs. The objective is to produce claims that survive contact with skeptical analysis.

Local inference and model choice

The language-model component of the system is deliberately constrained. It is not the forecaster. It is the orchestrator for hypothesis drafting, feature schema suggestion, experimental logging, and report synthesis. All scoring, event construction, statistical testing, and backtest logic remain deterministic and inspectable.

That design permits local execution. In lightweight mode, the orchestration layer can run with aggressively quantized models and heavy CPU offloading. In practical terms, that means the pipeline can be structured to work on modest local machines rather than requiring hosted inference for every reasoning step.

The important caveat is throughput. A “70B-class on 4GB” story only makes sense under extremely aggressive quantization and offloading assumptions, and even then the result is a constrained orchestration path rather than comfortable full-speed local inference. The usable engineering takeaway is narrower and more honest:

Large-model orchestration can be made local enough for research workflows, provided the LLM is used for structure and iteration rather than for every numerical task, and provided the statistical core of the system remains independent of model inference.

That separation of concerns is a feature, not a compromise. If a model hallucinates a filing interpretation, the backtest and audit stack should still reject the derived hypothesis cleanly.

What this is not

It is not a bot that takes two indicators and flips long or short. It is not a “market predictor” trained to map recent returns onto next-period returns. It is not an opaque reinforcement learning loop rewarded on simulated profit. Those systems can be fun; they are usually weak research instruments.

This project is better understood as an information-to-hypothesis compiler. Its central claim is that if you treat public policy data with the same rigor one would use in an academic research pipeline — controlled inputs, explicit assumptions, reproducible experiments, honest logging of negative results — you can build a much more interesting engine for alpha discovery than a hard-coded trading bot will ever be.

Why this direction matters

The open-source quant ecosystem has spent years saturating the same search space: price-only features, standard models, crowded backtests, and little attention to data provenance or information timing. The likely path to genuinely differentiated work is therefore not to squeeze a slightly better model out of the same candles, but to shift the research object altogether.

Public policy records are not obscure because they are useless. They are obscure because they are difficult. Difficulty is often a more promising sign than popularity.

Current state

The engine is currently being designed from the inside out:

Phase 1 Source connectors and raw storage. Preserve provenance and timing before interpretation.
Phase 2 Issuer graph and entity resolution. Build a trustworthy mapping layer before backtests.
Phase 3 Event feature library. Convert disclosures into lag-aware signals and interaction terms.
Phase 4 Hypothesis engine and audit memory. Turn experiments into cumulative research rather than isolated runs.

In other words: the project is being built as a research instrument first and a product second.

This post describes a research system for event-driven market analysis and alpha discovery. It is not financial advice and it is not a claim of production trading readiness. The central contribution is methodological: treating public policy information as a structured, auditable input to quant research rather than as anecdotal context around price.
